Dynamic & Static types

The mental ring (Ego &Superego; elements 1-4) of a Static type is formed from static elements, with their vital ring being formed by dynamic elements (Id &Super ID; elements 5-8). whereas Dynamic types are formed from dynamic phases with vital rings formed by static elements. Generally, static elements are more rigid and more or less fixed in a significant period of time (without movement). They are more inclined to say how stages ‘A, B, or C’ as they perceive the world in episodes.

However, Dynamic elements are changing and are constantly evolving as they perceive the world in a constant flow of time. Static types are people of ‘place’ and are constants that won’t change value or properties; Dynamic types are people of ‘time’ and are variables who are easily adaptable. This leads up to the point that Dynamic types are also known to be more contrived and observant, prioritising social context first as they consciously think about their actions instead of going by natural instinct. They are more focused on where and how events will eventually lead up to– Hence the reason why they are people of ‘time’. Static types like to place emphasis on the properties and values of events and are generally inclined to talk of properties and structures in reality. They move much more naturally and instinctually than Dynamic types without much thought on how others will perceive them, with their actions being based on external stimuli.

A Dynamic type’s mental ring is Dynamic and their vital ring is Static, a Dynamic type will talk about concrete and objective aspects and information of the external reality, actively interacting with concrete knowledge in the physical reality and contemplating the abstract information in the background. An example would be of an LSE– with their Te base being dynamic, this means that they focus on tasks or immediate situations and will consolidate it with conceptual knowledge backed by the vital ring sitting there passively until needed. Static types actively contemplate conceptual knowledge and interact with concrete knowledge in the background. Static types are generally more mobile than Dynamic types. The mental ring of a Static type aligns with the object’s form and inner content, to desires. In contrast, the mental ring of a Dynamic type concentrates on what is happening to and within the object including the object’s situation in time and space.

“External programming is received by the vital ring, which is dynamic for a static type and static for a dynamic type.” Therefore, to dynamic types, you simply inform them about the absence of objects and they'll get it themselves, if you say the opposite like ‘go get the water’, they'll be confused since they need to understand why they should act, whereas you should only inform static types about how to get an object, so they immediately understand that the object's absence thus needed. If you tell a static type that ‘there is no water’ they won’t realise that you’re hinting at them to go get water.

The Mental Ring & Vital Ring

The Mental ring and Vital ring dichotomy can be categorised as the conscious and unconscious body, with the Mental ring being the conscious body and the Vital ring being the unconscious body. The Mental ring consists of functions 1-4 (Base, Creative, Role, Vulnerable) while the Vital ring consists of functions 5-8 (Suggestive, Mobilising, Ignoring, Demonstrative). For example, an SLE’s (ESTp) elements would be; Se, Ti, Ne, Fi, Ni, Fe, Si, Te.

The Mental ring actively perceives information that enters our psyche, allowing us to constantly interact with it; therefore it is considered the conscious body. You are most aware of how you utilise or don’t utilise the functions making up the Mental ring and how it plays into your self-perception and your role in society, actively using it in conscious thought, conversations, logical frameworks, etc... The Mental ring is employed to offer advice, management, and supervision– Think of it as the main control centre of the brain, being employed on the frontlines to constantly guide us and give us insight, this is where we get our relations and contributions with the external world from.

On the other hand, the Vital ring takes a more passive approach, therefore we need external stimuli for us to pay attention to it. It is generally considered as the unconscious body, meaning that it automatically gets triggered without our conscious effort, rendering it as part of the background to work on securing our basic needs. Our unconsciousness contains Jungian archetypes (innate patterns of thought and behaviour seeking to manifest in an external reality) that are evoked from our environment, bonding the activity of the Vital ring with the body.

The way that the Mental and Vital ring interacts with each other is of one where the Vital ring consolidates information received from the Mental ring, as the Mental ring focuses on information and the Vital ring focuses on the implementation that arises from that information. The Vital ring reacts to the knowledge (can also be seen as ‘motivation’) given by the Mental ring and sets itself to work on creating an outcome, which then creates new experiences that imparts new information that essentially creates a ‘loop’ as the Mental ring will always perceive and the Vital ring will always respond by adapting the information into the external environment.

Information Metabolism Elements (IMEs)

Information Metabolism Elements (also known as IMEs) are subjectively perceived information surrounding us that are grouped into the 8 elements that make up a person’s cognitive framework (which is how the three letter typings is allocated and determined). The material world can be distinguished by substance (what is dense) and field (what cannot be touched). Substance are interactable things with boundaries that separates a distinct object from another; the end of an object. Therefore, we can divide substance into objects with their own properties and space between them.

“Field”, as defined by Ozhogov’s Explanatory Dictionary, is a space within the action of certain forces manifests (specialised use). Magnetic field. Force field. Gravitational field. We interpret field forces as energy, meaning that objects exist in a dynamic space filled with active forces. Nothing in this system is static; objects, space, and energy are in a constant state of flux. We perceive this continuous change as time, which we conveniently record as a sequence of events—like a seed sprouting, growing a trunk, and eventually becoming a full tree.

In SSS, Information Elements are; ‘work’, ‘system’, ‘comfort’, ‘relationships’, ‘emotions’, ‘opportunities’, ‘will’, ‘events’. How these elements are categorised are by 4 macro elements:

These elements are also categorised by their ‘Essential Component’-- Defining properties by its fundamental qualities, what distinguishes the objects from others, and their ‘Relational Component– The comparison and correlation of essential components with one another. Through these components, we are able to divide the macro elements as 2 elements per each macro element.

The IMEs that make up the mental framework of an individual are simplified into a three letter typing system; Extrovert/Introvert, Sensing/Intuitive, and Ethical/Logical. The positions of these dichotomies in the three letter system determine the rationality and irrationality of an individual. Individuals with base judging IMEs (Te/Ti/Fe/Fi) are considered rational whereas individuals with base perceiving IMEs (Ne/Ni/Si/Se) are considered as irrational. Rationality in socionics is a perceptual quality defined by a focus on actions and emotions. In contrast, Irrationality means a focus on states of mind and body.

It tracks the kinetic movement of the object in space, which includes events, changes in position in space, and actions. With the information given from its perception of the object’s actions, it helps the subject assess situations alongside objective and factual information. Whether the object is real or virtual doesn’t matter as we can identify the properties of abstract objects (The qualities of the human psyche). Therefore, the exclusive definition of Te is that it is singled out from the environment and possesses specific qualities, properties, and attributes that we use in daily activity.

Te is an element that deals with acceleration (the rate of change of velocity– speed of something within a given direction) as it is a vector element concerning itself on the employment of kinetic energy derived and supplied by Se– Therefore, we can interpret Te as the following facets accepted by SHS, SWS, SCS;

Ti (Introverted Logic– “System”); Perception of objects’ external standings with each other

Ti is a rational, static, and introverted element part of the macroelement; “Objects”. Ti deals with the relations of properties and attributes within objects, in order to organise it in a system (a total of established distances as a result of external movements) based on identification of the properties, attributes, and qualities of objects. The comparison of properties within two or more objects. It is to find common properties; patterns within objects and determine the distance between them and not based on quantity.

Ti is the objective comparison of qualities compared to Fi’s subjective comparison of qualities to determine the relations between objects. To look at a cat and a lion and objectively state that the lion is bigger than the cat is Ti.

In SCS, Ti is associated with objective needs for an individual as objective needs can be defined as fundamental needs required for one to live, which can be interpreted as an object having power held over you. The comparison between objects is a power hierarchy which links back to Ti.

Fe (Extroverted Feeling– “Emotion”); Perception of kinetic energy within objects.

Fe is a rational, dynamic, and extroverted element, part of the macroelement; “Energy”. The world is composed of potential energy, in which it is transformed into kinetic energy. For example, how you feel (Happy? Sad?) manifests as physical behaviour where your muscles move in (kinetic energy) correspondence of emotions such as smiling or crying. Kinetic energy corresponds to emotionality in the sense that whenever you’re happy, you feel your energy levels increase. Whenever you feel sad, your energy levels decrease. Emotions essentially are “energy in motion”, which manifests as physical sensations and physiological responses. Therefore, the change to kinetic energy allows one to perceive internal energetic states, vitality, and emotionality within objects and themselves.

Fe is a dynamic IME, so it takes in and perceives the information from an object’s internal dynamics and the changes happening within it— One’s mood, change in state of emotion, etc. Determining one’s ability or inability to control one’s own emotional state and the emotional state of others. Fe can produce emotional contagion within others (unconscious tendency to mimic and synchronize with the emotions and behaviors of others, leading to the "catching" of feeling), as a surge in emotions can lead to physical expressions in which the left over energy is transferred over to another object. The Fe base is able to impact others emotions and moods based on what the Fe base considers beneficial for them.

Fi (Introverted Feeling— “Relationships”); Perception of attitudes of objects towards each other.

Fi is a rational, static, and introverted element of the macroelement; “Energy”. Fi is the subjective comparison of energetic states in relations to objects, always needing to have potential/kinetic energy directed towards an object. For example, the presence of an object can affect one’s state of emotion (someone makes you feel annoyed), which could be induced by a characteristic/mannerism of theirs or simply previous negative experiences. One has already established a relationship with this individual where it is marked by discomfort or unease during interactions. The opposite can be experienced, where one can experience pleasantness by another object and develop a “feel-good” relation with it.

The comparison of such relations (ranging from bad to good) helps create a framework of pleasant, unpleasant, attraction, repulsion, people, objects. This can determine whether an object needs another object; the lack of mutual or unilateral needs. Fi obtains information regarding objects that satisfy their desires and interests, such as feelings of like and dislike, love and hate, etc.. SCS states that higher feelings of the kind are considered “ethical” due to how the interrelation between people’s needs are mostly regulated by ethical norms.

Differentiation between how Si evokes feeling compared to how Fi evokes emotion within one’s self when perceiving the relations of an object is that one is a sensoric feeling (e.g; I’m feeling cold) whereas one is attraction/repulsion. In SSS, the feeling you get from witnessing harmonic lines is from a recognition of beauty and the pleasant experience it gives us. Whether or not this creates attractive energy is another matter. It should be remembered that when a TIM perceives the world, it does not receive only one piece of information at a time, but a complex package of multiple elements that are processed simultaneously (this is why beauty and attraction can come intertwine in someone's dialogue at the same time, yet one could be simply referring to it from a pure aesthetic point of view). Attraction results in the interaction between two different energies in the sense of a person informing you emotionally, verbally, visually, etc.. in which a relationship will be created.

Se (Extroverted Sensing– “Will”); The perception of an object’s external appearance

Se is an irrational, static, and extroverted element part of the macroelement; “Space”. Space is the area between objects defined by their boundaries. When there is a boundary, there is measurable space, and within that space are objects. In order to change (restrict) a boundary, one applies force, and the objects in that space are impacted. Therefore, the element “Will” is connected to force. These boundaries can be expanded, reduced, preserved, safeguarded, etc.. The possession of space, and space itself, isn’t only physical– but psychological or virtual. For example, to accumulate wealth is to expand or reinforce one’s space. Possession of space and its dimensions can not only be achieved through force, but with control of the space and the objects within it.

Se concerns itself with the perception of an object’s external appearance, and Se is kinetic energy itself. Why? External appearance is one of the qualities of Se, receiving information you perceive from an object. Through this assessment, they determine the quality and ability to exercise their will onto the object, exercising kinetic energy onto the object from assessing them.

People can easily confuse Se and Te together as they both involve kinetic energy. However, there is an important distinction to make. Although Te and Se do have some overlap for external behaviours, they have different motivations as the IMEs are different in nature. Kinetic energy refers to the force of an object. For regular external objects, non-living things, this could be the force of a rock falling into the ground (raw kinetic energy; Se). For living things such as humans, kinetic force may be described as our capacity to exert force both mentally & physically. In order to diffrentiate Se from Te, it is important to note that Te is focused on the physical movement of an object and its work displayed. As we can infer from the physics formula, W = F x S (displacement), work cannot be accomplished without a certain will/”willingness”. Therefore, the distinction is between the intent of the element. Te manipulates kinetic energy as a tool while Se uses kinetic energy as an end goal.

This provides information about the sequence of events and people’s actions, the causal interdependence and people’s feelings caused by this interdependence. Horizontal blocks

There are 4 horizontal blocks, Ego, Super-Ego, ID, and Super-ID; with each block containing one Accepting and Producing element. Each block is viewed as a unit performing a certain type of work for the entire model. The mental ring consists of the ego and superego blocks (Base (1), Creative (2), Role (3), PoLR (4)), the vital ring consists of the super-id and id blocks (Suggestive (5), Mobilising (6), Ignoring (7), Demonstrative (8)). These 4 horizontal blocks describe how different IMEs manifest within the 16 sociotypes.

Ego and Id are executive blocks while Superego and Super-id are control blocks. Some may view it queer that low dimensional functions are control blocks and high dimensional functions are executive blocks. However, this is as control is only a signal to action the same way the PoLR, take instance, is insecure enough that one wishes to overcompensate it, leading to it being the deciding factor to execute out an action.

Ego; Base (1), Creative (2)

The ego block is fully independent and creative, being the individual's creative self-realisation to consciously influence the external world. The Ego block isn't about finding one "right" or objective answer. Its main goal is to get what it desires. Picking and choosing which facts it needs to fulfil its desire, ignoring others. It will rearrange the information until it finds a way to reach its goal that one feels is reasonably honest and fair.

Superego; Role (3), PoLR (4)

They are socially conforming and informational, trying to copy the objective picture of the external world as all known facts are equal. It tries to fit in and fulfil the expectations of others. Production of Superego is normative, and each premise only leads to one conclusion that doesn’t change depending on the situation.

The mental track is the superblock of social life where one goes through the crisis of teenagehood, and learns that he is not the only one with his own interests and needs. One must include self activity with the life activities of others– people and society.

Super-id; Suggestive (5), Mobilising (6)

Super-id perceives information regarding whether it is successfully functioning or unsuccessfully functioning. Due to the “mine vs not mine” dichotomy, the block prioritises its own interests over others, being apprehensive whenever they need to let their own interests go. The need for “self-sacrifice” is to gain the attention of others and for others to hopefully disallow them to “self-sacrifice”. If their attempt is not noticed or they aren't able to earn “corrective” marks, then they are tormented by anti-conscience. The feeling that others around you are guilty of not having noticed your “selfless” attempt to “self-sacrifice”.

In SSS, according to V.D Yermak, the Super-id (vital track superblock) is filled with information (experience, norms, programs) and undergoes training til 14, so the attention dominant within children shifts to Super-id

Id; Ignoring (7), Demonstrative (8)

The Id is self-realising, being the block of aspirations, desires, and strivings. The Id often carries unconscious family patterns, causing us to react to certain triggers without knowing the reason and the source of the behaviour. The Id appears to be furthest from conscious awareness, with a sparse number of people having few memories before the age of 3. Essentially, the environment you grew up in during early childhood served as the foundational "programming" for your deepest instincts and needs.

Dimensions

Every element used by a subject has a dimension determining the level of depth and nuance to it. There are 4 dimensions total housing 4 parameters;

  • Experience (Ex); Characteristics of individual or social experience from birth & developed by one during their life in the process of decision-making & execution of control actions.
  • Norms (Nr); Characteristics of norms, rules, customs, accepted techniques & standards (individual or social) that is followed during the process of decision making or execution of control actions.
  • Situations (St); Characteristics of conditions and your environment (individual or social) to condition your process of decision making or execution of control actions.
  • Time (Tm); Parametric time. A moment in time in the past, present, or future relative to which an individual or social decision is made or executed.

For every dimension that comes in tandem to the prior dimension, the parameters are built up like layers. This means that the 1st dimension is the dimension with the least depth as it houses only one parameter– Experience. The 2nd dimension builds upon the 1st dimension, including Experience and Norms. This continues so on and so forth until we reach the 4th dimension in which it houses all 4 parameters. The amount of dimensions in a element indicates the number of parameters taken into account when the element is in usage. The more dimensional an element the better information is compiled as the range for its usage is wider. This allows a more adequate result to be produced (hence why low dimensional elements are clumsy in usage and vice versa). Each dimension hosts 2 elements in total, 1 element from the Mental and 1 from the Vital ring.

  • 1st Dimension; Experience
  • 2nd Dimension; Experience and norms
  • 3rd Dimension; Experience, norms, and situations
  • 4th Dimension; Experience, norms, situations, and time

The 1st Dimension

The 1st Dimension is where the lowest dimensional functions reside, hosting the Suggestive function (5) and the Vulnerable function (4). They only have one parameter; Experience, which is our perception of the world. As the Experience parameter (Ex) is the foundation for where the rest of the parameters are built up, the 1st Dimension doesn’t host the rest of the parameters that fleshes it out. With each processing of information, a person receives experience. Therefore, it only relies on one’s self-perception and is unable to empathise nor understand others’ perspective, nor is one able to convey their perception of experience. One can describe what they experience and state the existence of their experience, and it will be similar to the actual thing, but it is impossible for them to explain the true nature of the experience; only a fragmented understanding of what it is. You can eat an apple and describe to a tasteless person your experience of eating an apple, but the tasteless person won’t know the actual taste and sensation of the apple. He is only able to use his imagination to understand what an apple might taste like, but he has never experienced such taste so words such as ‘sweet’ & ‘acidic’ are inherently meaningless to him.

The 4th & 5th function only draws upon experience when processing information, and they are indifferent to norms, situation, and time. Their usage is only based on social/individual experience, so to an observer it may come off as queer. The person will be unaware that their usage of the dimension is queer as they do not have a comparison tool (Nr), unable to evaluate themselves from the ‘outside’. However, they are aware that they are unable to use the norms of a 1D function to their advantage, often having the feeling of helplessness when in a new situation (as they have no experience to compare to) with the inability to make a decision. In a society, there are rules and norms drilled into the people and the environment. The ‘rights’ & ‘wrongs’ of how you should do a particular action.

This is not to mistake the fact that the person has no understanding whatsoever of the norms related to the function, as one often has a general understanding of the underlying norms that comes with the function. However, in practice– when one is forced to use their 1D function is one then unable to apply their knowledge of the norms associated with the function. Each new situation is a new experience, therefore they can only grasp upon their previous experiences/feelings to execute an action. If one is extremely uncomfortable with usage of their 1D function or lacks experience then they will turn to the adjacent, multidimensional function. For example, an LSI with 1D Fe who is faced with a situation where they need to comfort a loved one might turn to physical resources (Se) to comfort them, such as bringing a cup of tea as comfort.

Usually, a person seeks external validation about how suitable their responses are relating to their 1D functions. This is because one lives in a society, and society follows a normative, dichotomous guide on what is “right” & “wrong”; whether someone is dressed “indecently” or “decently”, has a “moral” or “immoral” view, etc.. Therefore, one is sensitive about criticism towards their 1D function, as usage is “clumsy” and “unrefined”, seeking approval from others. If a 1D function accumulates too much criticism then one will develop an inferiority complex. Often, one is hit with questions such as “What needs to be done?” “What is the correct solution?”. One does not have any norms to turn to when seeking an answer, therefore they will come up with their own norms to confront such questions, making their perception of the function abstract and unique.

‘Any information that ends up in a 1D function is assessed by the function as pleasant or unpleasant. Natural (from the viewpoint of the 1D function) reaction when encountering an “unpleasant” information is the desire to get rid of it, to turn away, not to take it into account, to pretend that it does not exist, to forget, to fence off in any way.’ – School of System Socionics.

When one encounters an unpleasant situation, their innate instinct is to get rid of it as quickly as possible. They panic in their haste to lose the problem, their thinking becoming shallow and narrow as they deliberately avoid anything that does not concern the immediate goal. One trips through bumps on the dirt road as they can only see what is directly in front of them, blinded by tunnel vision; almost as if one were a provoked bull. One can only see the red flag in front of them, and in this crucial situation that involves their 1D function, do they react quickly without much thought– running towards the red flag to make good riddance of it.     The only exception to this dichotomy is the Base function (1) as it is defined as the will of an individual with a profound amount of self awareness. It strives to act as a motivating force that compels one to draw their own conclusions. As the base function, it has a relatively more nuanced and complex understanding in comparison to every other element, influencing the entire psyche.

    Valued & Unvalued

    Valued functions are functions strong in Mental and weak in Vital; Base (1), Creative (2), Suggestive (5), Mobilising (6), whereas unvalued functions are weak in Mental and strong in Vital; Role (3), PoLR (4), Ignoring (7), Demonstrative (8).

    Valued functions perceive, processes, and assimilate information– without resistance –that forms our world-view, allowing us to interpret many phenomena through them. Unvalued functions limit excess information, being disinterested in topics concerned with them as one sees them through opposing aspects. These functions are seen as purely for utility and maintenance, but are not counted on to express nor realise one’s self.
